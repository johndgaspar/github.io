The story behind
the strategy

Most marketing strategists learn consumer behavior from a textbook. I spent years sitting across from real people — understanding their fears, their motivations, the gap between what they say and what they actually do. That's not a credential. That's a completely different way of seeing an audience.

My path wasn't linear. I trained as a clinical psychologist, earning my M.A. from Hofstra University and my M.S. from Angelo State, where I also taught research methods, published peer-reviewed work, and led National Science Foundation research into how people make decisions. Then I pivoted — deliberately — into marketing strategy, because I saw that the brands winning right now aren't just the ones with the biggest budgets. They're the ones that actually understand people.

I'm currently completing my MBA in Marketing Analytics at Texas Tech University (top 20% of my cohort), while running aMiGO — an AI-powered wellness platform I built from zero, including 25+ customer discovery interviews, competitive analysis, and a full go-to-market strategy.

I'm bilingual English/Spanish at a native level, which isn't just a language skill — it's a cultural insight into one of the fastest-growing consumer markets in the US. I'm relocating to Austin in 2026, where I intend to build the kind of strategy work that actually moves people. Eventually, my own agency. For now, let's build something together.

What clinical psychology taught me about brand positioning

Jonathan D. Gonzalez Gaspar · March 2026 · Strategy & Psychology

In therapy, there's a concept called the therapeutic alliance — the relationship between a clinician and a client that research consistently shows predicts outcomes more than any specific technique. In other words, how you make someone feel matters more than what you do to them.

I spent years building that skill. Understanding what people actually need versus what they say they need. Holding space for the gap between stated behavior and real behavior. Watching people resist exactly what would help them, for reasons they couldn't articulate.

Then I entered marketing. And I saw the same gap, everywhere — between what brands say to their audiences and what their audiences actually need to hear.

The problem with most brand strategy

Most brand positioning starts with the product. Here's what we make. Here's why it's good. Here's why you should want it. This is backwards. It's the equivalent of a therapist walking into session and immediately telling a client what their problem is. Technically you might be right. But you haven't earned the right to be heard — and without that, nothing you say lands.

The brands that actually win start with the person. Not the demographic. Not the persona card on a conference room wall. The actual emotional reality of someone's life — what they're afraid of, what they're proud of, what gap they're trying to close. Brand positioning is really a listening problem dressed up as a messaging problem.

What behavioral science actually teaches us

One of the most durable findings in psychology is that people are remarkably poor predictors of their own behavior. We say we make decisions rationally. We don't. We say we choose based on features and price. We don't. We choose based on identity, social proof, loss aversion, and the story a product lets us tell about ourselves.

This isn't new information — behavioral economists have been writing about it for decades. But most marketing teams treat it as an interesting footnote, not a foundational design principle. Consumer insight that stops at "people want to feel good" is about as useful as a clinical assessment that stops at "patient seems stressed."

The real work is going deeper. What specifically triggers that emotional response? What are the competing motivations? What does this person believe about themselves — and how does your brand either confirm or challenge that belief?

The bilingual dimension

I want to name something that rarely gets discussed directly in marketing: the difference between translating for a market and actually understanding it. As a native Spanish speaker working in the US, I've watched brands spend enormous budgets on Spanish-language campaigns that feel hollow to the communities they're targeting — because they were built by people who see "Hispanic" as a demographic checkbox, not a lived identity with specific cultural textures, values, and humor.

Authentic bilingual strategy isn't translation. It's cultural fluency. It's knowing when the Spanish version of your campaign should feel different — not just sound different. It's understanding that "familia" carries emotional weight in ways that "family" in English often doesn't, and building creative around that weight rather than around the word.

Where this leaves me

I didn't become a marketing strategist despite my psychology background. I became one because of it. The years I spent learning how people think, how they resist, how they change — those are the years that let me see what most brand strategy misses.

Strategy built on genuine human understanding doesn't just perform better on a dashboard. It earns the kind of loyalty that compounds over time — because it treats people like people, not conversion events.

That's the work I want to do. And it's just getting started.
